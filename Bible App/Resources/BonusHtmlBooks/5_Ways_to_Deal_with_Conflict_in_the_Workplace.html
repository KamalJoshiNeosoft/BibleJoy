5 Ways to Deal with Conflict in the Workplace

 

Working alongside someone means they see you at your best, your worst, and many other times in between.  There are times that co-workers share laughter, deep discussions, or joy-filled celebrations. Nonetheless, there are other times that stress replaces unity and civility. How can someone deal with conflict in the workplace while continuing to display Christlike attributes?

 

  1. Walk away.  When our emotions are at their highest level, it is best to walk away.  Go for a walk.  Go back inside your office or remain quiet.  Speaking from one’s most emotional state can lead to saying harsh things that are not how we will really feel in hours (or even minutes) later.  “Death and life are in the power of the tongue, and those who love it will eat its fruits” (Proverbs 18:21).

 

  1. Pray.  It is always a good idea to pray when stress is involved.  Pray for discernment.  Pray for wisdom.  Pray for the other people involved in the conflict.  Seeking God’s wisdom and assistance in the situation removes our carnal limitations.  We are surrendering our inabilities to Him.  In the Sermon on the Mount, Jesus told us:  “Pray for those who persecute you.”  Sometimes we have to accept that our poor decisions lead to our own selfish persecutions.  So, don’t get caught up in praying for the other person’s thoughts to change.  Pray instead for resolution. 

 

  1. Don’t focus on whose right or wrong.  While it may not be immediately clear what the resolution to the conflict is, focusing on who is right or who is wrong will only intensify the argument.  All too often, we want to think someone has to be right or wrong during a conflict.  This should be the least important part of conflict resolution.  Work to solve the problem and remember that people who like or even love each other still argue at times.  In Psalm 141:3, we are reminded to allow the Lord to work through our words.  In this case, accept that conflict occurs as a normal part of day to day living, but that does not mean anyone has to be labeled as the winner of an argument.

 

  1. Remember that people can disagree and still like each other.  When we get upset with someone, are we able to rationally discuss the conflict?  Or, do we immediately attach that person’s value in our lives to the conflict?  Life is filled with conflict and while some of those conflicts occur with people we do not know, the majority of them occur with people who are very dear to us.  Remember this the next time, you find yourself in a conflict at work (or anywhere).  A conflict may cause strong emotions or uncomfortableness, but it does not mean that the person you ate lunch with yesterday is more valuable than the person you are arguing with 24-hours later.  Romans 14:18 advises, “....Make every effort to do what leads to peace and mutual edification.”

 

  1. Be at Peace.  Romans 12:18 reads, “If possible, so far as it depends, live peaceably with all.”  If the conflict is serious, continue doing what you can to maintain civility.  Be a peacemaker and continue praying, working, and seeking God’s wisdom.  In doing this, allow Christ’s light and peace to radiate from you even during conflicting times.
