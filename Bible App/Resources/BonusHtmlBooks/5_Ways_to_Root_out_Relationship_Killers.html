5 Ways to Root out Relationship Killers

 

When people invest in each other, there are going to be misunderstandings, conflicts, and even general times of feeling disconnected. This is true for married couples, friendships, family members, and work relationships. In a world where emotions are high and schedules are intense, what are 5 ways to root out relationship killers?

 

  1. Physical absence?  Don't make it about you.  ¨With all humility and gentleness, with patience, bearing with one another in love¨ (Ephesians 4:2).  This tip has different applications.  When a loved one feels distant, it is impossible that they are going through something and their absence has nothing to do with you.  This does not give the other person a chance to skip out on their responsibilities, but when the other person in a relationship goes aloof, try to think about what might be going on to create this distance.  If there have not been any disagreements or other problems, don't just assume that the person is upset with you. 

 

  1. Snarky replies?  Don't make it about you.  ¨A friend loves at all times, and a brother is born for adversity¨ (Proverbs 17:17).  When stress overwhelms our lives, it is easy to lash out the most inappropriately at the people whom we love and cherish the most.  Some people would liken this to the children's novel, The Whipping Boy.  When you truly care about someone, it is easy to take our bad moods, our patches out on those people whom we know love us and accept us at our best.  Now, clearly, being snarky or snide with someone is not okay and it is not how God wants us to respond, but when foul words come (as they most always do), try to put yourself in the other person's place.  Rather than judging them and trying to force an apology which can lead to more conflict, look for ways to help the other person.  Maybe what they need is someone to take something off their plate or maybe they just are mentally, emotionally, or physically unable to be present at that moment.

 

  1. Be honest.  ¨Do not lie to one another, seeing that you have put off the old self with its practices¨ (Collosians 3:9).  One of the greatest gifts we can provide in a relationship is honesty.  Wait until emotions have calmed and then be honest.  Express yourself in a relationship with honesty, sincerity, and truth. 

 

  1. Don´t Rush It.  ¨Do not be anxious about anything, but in everything by prayer and supplication with thanksgiving let your requests be made known to God. And the peace of God, which surpasses all understanding, will guard your hearts and your minds in Christ Jesus¨ (Phillippians 4:6-7).  When relationships have problems, solutions can be rushed.  Try to not push a solution.  Pray for the other person.  Offer a kind word, an encouraging deed, and an apology (if needed), but don't force it.  Sometimes people need time. 

 

  1. Pray.  ¨Therefore, confess your sins to one another and pray for one another, that you may be healed. The prayer of a righteous person has great power as it is working¨ (James 5:16).  I once read a motivational statement that said the greatest gift we can give someone is the mention of them in our prayers.  Allow your relationship to be grounded in prayer.  Pray through the pain, the misunderstandings, the good times, and all times in between.  Praying for our friends is a sure way to root out relationship killers!
