8 Ways to Respond to a Grieving Friend

 

When we see people we love hurting, our natural response is to want to help. When friends are grieving the loss of a loved one, the natural question is: what can I do to help?

 

Psalm 23 is a beautiful scripture that many of us can likely quote word for word….¨The Lord is my shepherd I shall not want, He maketh me to lie down in green pastures, he leads me beside the still water, He restores my soul, He guides me in paths of righteousness for his name's sake, Even though I will walk through the valley of the shadow of death, I will fear no evil, for you are with me, your rod and your staff….they comfort me. You prepare a table before me in presence of my enemies, You anoint my head with oil, my cup overflows, Surely goodness and love with follow me all the days of my life and I will dwell in the house of the Lord forever…..¨

 

Within this scripture are some words and actions that we can heed to help a friend who is grieving.

 

  1.  Sometimes we want to be there for that person and there is a time and place for that, but they may also need time and space.  Don´t push yourself on them, but also don't be offended if they can´t talk, respond to your messages, or answer the door when you knock. 

 

  1. Pray for your friend.  Pray for them my name and ask the Lord to be near to them. 

 

  1. Remind your friend that they belong to someone who has overcome death and the grave.  A gentle reminder that a reunion with loved ones who have passed is certain. 

 

  1. Point them to Jesus.  If your friend is a Christian, this is obviously easier, but even if they are not, find ways to direct them to God without seeming too preachy or pushy.  Let them see Jesus in you. 

 

  1. Give them permission to grieve.  Depending on how close you are to the friend, you may be there when they have their biggest meltdown or not.  They may make comments about needing to be okay and being unable to cry.  Giving them the freedom to cry and grieve is a useful tool during this process.  Remind them that God wants us to bring our burdens before Him.

 

  1. When the time is right, invite your friend to dinner.  Allow them to know up front that there is no pressure and no obligation to accept.

 

  1. Let them lead the conversation.  When someone we know has passed away, it is natural for us to try to make that the center of conversation or to do the exact opposite and avoid that person's name at all costs.  Your friend who is grieving may want to recount memories of their loved one, or they may simply need to set their grief to the side for the time being and live the moment.

 

  1. Remind yourself and your friend that grief comes in waves.  Your friend may not understand why they are standing strong and laughing one minute and then crying uncontrollably the next. This is the cycle of grief, and moments may change into days, but the bad moments can still creep in unexpectedly.  Offer reassurance that this is normal. 

 

As you go into the valley of grief with your friend, remember that you cannot feel what they feel.  You may have had a similar experience in the past, but your distance from that moment may make your response different from their immediate one.  Use this opportunity to practice 1 Peter 5:7:  ¨Cast all your anxiety on him because he cares for you.¨
